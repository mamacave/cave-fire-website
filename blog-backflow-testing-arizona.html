Backflow Preventer Testing in Arizona: Requirements, What's Tested & How to Stay Compliant

By Cave Fire Protection LLC  ·  May 5, 2026  ·  5 min read

If your commercial property in Arizona has a fire sprinkler system, irrigation system, or any other connection to the public water supply — you almost certainly have a backflow preventer, and Arizona requires it to be tested annually. It's one of the most overlooked compliance items for property managers, and failing to test can result in notices from your water utility, failed inspections, and potential service interruption.

Here's what you need to know.

What Is a Backflow Preventer?

A backflow preventer is a mechanical device installed on your water supply lines that stops contaminated water from flowing backwards into the public water supply. Without it, chemicals, bacteria, or other contaminants from your building's system could enter the municipal water supply under certain pressure conditions.

For fire sprinkler systems specifically, the backflow preventer sits between the city water main and your sprinkler riser. It protects the public water supply from the stagnant, potentially contaminated water that sits inside your fire sprinkler pipes.

Important: Backflow preventer testing for fire systems is a separate requirement from your NFPA 25 sprinkler inspection. Both are required annually, but they're governed by different standards and reported to different agencies.

Arizona Backflow Testing Requirements

In Arizona, backflow preventer testing requirements are enforced at the local water utility level. Cities like Phoenix, Scottsdale, Mesa, Chandler, Goodyear, and others all require annual testing of backflow prevention assemblies connected to the public water supply.

Key requirements include:

  • Annual testing by a licensed backflow tester certified by the state or local authority
  • Test results submitted to the water utility on an approved form
  • Failed devices must be repaired and retested within a specified timeframe (typically 30 days)
  • Records must be maintained by the property owner

Non-compliance can result in notices from your water utility and, in some cases, water service interruption until the device is tested and results are submitted.

Types of Backflow Preventers Commonly Found in Phoenix-Area Properties

Reduced Pressure Zone (RPZ) Assembly

The most common type for fire sprinkler systems and high-hazard applications. RPZ assemblies provide the highest level of protection and are required by most Arizona water utilities for fire service connections. They have two independent check valves and a relief valve in between.

Double Check Valve Assembly (DCVA)

Used for lower-hazard applications such as irrigation systems. Less common for fire sprinkler service but sometimes found in older installations.

Pressure Vacuum Breaker (PVB)

Typically used for irrigation only. Not approved for fire suppression system connections.

What Happens During a Backflow Test?

The test itself typically takes 15–30 minutes per device. Here's what to expect:

  • The tester will connect a differential pressure gauge (test kit) to the test cocks on the assembly
  • Valves are closed and readings are taken on each check valve and the relief valve
  • Results are recorded on a test report form
  • If the device passes, the report is submitted to the water utility
  • If the device fails, it must be repaired or replaced and retested

Note: The test involves briefly shutting off water flow to your fire system. For occupied buildings, this should be coordinated in advance. Cave Fire Protection coordinates with your alarm monitoring company and notifies the fire department if required during the test window.

Bundling Backflow Testing with Your Annual Sprinkler Inspection

One of the most efficient approaches for Phoenix-area property managers is to bundle your annual backflow test with your NFPA 25 sprinkler inspection. Both are required annually, and scheduling them together means:

  • One site visit instead of two
  • One point of contact for both compliance documents
  • Coordinated shutdown window for the fire system
  • Potential cost savings vs. scheduling separately

What to Do If Your Backflow Preventer Fails

A failed backflow test doesn't mean your system is immediately out of service — but it does need to be addressed quickly. Common failure causes include:

  • Worn or fouled check valve seats (often caused by debris or mineral buildup)
  • Failed relief valve that opens under normal pressure
  • Damaged or corroded check valve discs

Most backflow assembly failures can be repaired by cleaning or replacing internal components without replacing the entire assembly. Cave Fire Protection carries common repair parts and can often complete repairs the same day as the failed test.
