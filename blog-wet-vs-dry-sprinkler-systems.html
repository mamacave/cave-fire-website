Wet vs. Dry Pipe Sprinkler Systems: What Arizona Property Owners Need to Know

By Cave Fire Protection LLC  ·  May 7, 2026  ·  6 min read

Most commercial buildings in the Phoenix Valley have a fire sprinkler system — but many property owners and managers don't know which type they have or what that means for how it's maintained. The two most common systems are wet pipe and dry pipe, and they behave very differently. Understanding the distinction helps you know what to expect during inspections, why certain maintenance tasks exist, and what conditions each system is designed for.

Wet Pipe Systems: The Standard

A wet pipe system is exactly what the name suggests — the sprinkler pipes are always filled with pressurized water. When a sprinkler head activates (triggered by heat, not smoke), water discharges immediately from that head only.

Wet pipe systems are the most common type in commercial buildings throughout Arizona and account for the large majority of installations. They are mechanically simple, respond quickly, and require less maintenance than their dry pipe counterparts.

Where wet pipe systems are used

  • Office buildings and retail spaces
  • Restaurants and hospitality properties
  • Medical offices and healthcare facilities
  • Multi-tenant commercial buildings
  • Schools and educational facilities
  • Any conditioned space where pipes won't freeze

What wet pipe maintenance involves

Because the system is always pressurized with water, the primary maintenance concerns are leaks, corrosion, and proper pressure. NFPA 25 requires annual inspection of sprinkler heads, control valves, gauges, and alarm devices. The main drain test — which briefly opens a drain valve to check water supply pressure — is performed annually and produces the characteristic gurgling or flushing sound some tenants find alarming the first time they hear it.

Dry Pipe Systems: Built for Specific Conditions

In a dry pipe system, the pipes are filled with pressurized air or nitrogen instead of water. Water is held back by a specialized valve called the dry pipe valve. When a sprinkler head activates, the air pressure drops, the valve opens, and water fills the pipes before discharging.

This design exists for one primary reason: to protect spaces where standing water in the pipes could freeze. In Arizona, that most commonly applies to unheated warehouses, parking structures, loading docks, cold-storage facilities, and attic spaces in certain building types.

Where dry pipe systems are used in Arizona

  • Warehouses and distribution centers with unheated sections
  • Cold-storage and refrigerated facilities
  • Covered parking structures exposed to temperature swings
  • Loading dock areas
  • Large attic spaces in certain commercial buildings
  • Any unheated space where temperatures could drop below 40°F

The trade-off: response time

The critical difference between wet and dry pipe systems is activation time. In a wet pipe system, water flows the moment a head activates. In a dry pipe system, there is a delay — typically 15 to 60 seconds — while air pressure releases and water travels through the pipes to the activated head. NFPA 13 limits this delivery time to 60 seconds for most applications.

In a fast-moving fire, that delay matters. Dry pipe systems are only specified where freeze protection genuinely requires it — not as a general preference.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Wet Pipe
  • Pipes always filled with water
  • Immediate water delivery on activation
  • Simpler design, fewer components
  • Lower maintenance requirements
  • Less susceptible to mechanical failure
  • Requires conditioned / heated space
  • Most common in Phoenix Valley buildings
Dry Pipe
  • Pipes filled with pressurized air/nitrogen
  • 15–60 second delay before water flows
  • Dry pipe valve, air compressor required
  • More frequent inspection and testing
  • Higher maintenance cost over time
  • Designed for unheated / freeze-risk spaces
  • Common in warehouses and cold storage

Inspection Differences Under NFPA 25

Both systems fall under NFPA 25, but dry pipe systems require additional testing. The most significant is the annual trip test — a controlled activation of the dry pipe valve that verifies it opens properly and water reaches the inspector's test connection within the required timeframe. This test requires coordination because it temporarily takes the system out of service and refilling/resetting the system afterward takes time.

Dry pipe systems also require more attention to the air supply components. The air compressor (or nitrogen supply), pressure gauges, and low air pressure alarms all need regular inspection. Gauge checks for dry and preaction systems are required weekly under NFPA 25, compared to monthly for wet pipe systems.

Corrosion: A Growing Concern in Both Types

Internal pipe corrosion is a documented problem in both wet and dry pipe systems, but it's significantly more aggressive in dry pipe systems. The presence of oxygen in the air charge, combined with moisture that enters when the system trips or is tested, creates conditions that accelerate microbiologically influenced corrosion (MIC) and oxygen-driven corrosion.

Symptoms include pinhole leaks, discolored water during draining, and unusual pressure loss. NFPA 25's five-year internal inspection requirement exists specifically to catch corrosion before it causes pipe failure. For dry pipe systems in Arizona, corrosion assessment is worth prioritizing — especially in systems more than 10 years old.

One mitigation option is nitrogen inerting — replacing the air charge with nitrogen, which is inert and doesn't support corrosion. Cave Fire Protection can evaluate whether nitrogen conversion makes sense for your dry pipe system.

Which System Does Your Property Have?

The easiest way to tell: look at your fire riser room. A wet pipe system will have a straightforward alarm check valve or a simple riser with a pressure gauge showing water pressure. A dry pipe system will have a larger, more complex valve assembly — the dry pipe valve — along with an air pressure gauge, an air supply line, and often a small compressor or nitrogen cylinder nearby.

If you're not sure, your annual inspection report should identify the system type. If you don't have a recent inspection report or you've inherited a building without documentation, Cave Fire Protection can assess and document your system during a site visit.
