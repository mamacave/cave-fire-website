NFPA 25 Inspection Requirements in Arizona: What Property Managers Need to Know

By Cave Fire Protection LLC  ·  May 5, 2026  ·  6 min read

If you manage a commercial property in Arizona — whether it's a restaurant in Phoenix, a warehouse in Goodyear, or a medical office in Scottsdale — your fire sprinkler system is subject to NFPA 25: Standard for the Inspection, Testing, and Maintenance of Water-Based Fire Protection Systems. Failing to comply isn't just a code violation. It can void your insurance, expose you to liability, and — most importantly — put people at risk.

Here's what Arizona property managers need to know about NFPA 25 requirements, inspection frequencies, and what a proper inspection actually involves.

What Is NFPA 25?

NFPA 25 is the national standard that governs how water-based fire protection systems — including wet pipe, dry pipe, and deluge sprinkler systems — must be inspected, tested, and maintained. In Arizona, the State Fire Marshal and local jurisdictions (like Phoenix, Mesa, and Scottsdale) adopt and enforce NFPA 25 as part of their fire codes.

The standard applies to virtually every commercial property with a fire sprinkler system: restaurants, retail stores, offices, warehouses, schools, hotels, medical facilities, and multifamily residential buildings.

NFPA 25 Inspection Frequencies

One of the most common questions we hear from property managers is: "How often does my system need to be inspected?" The answer depends on the component being inspected.

Annual inspections are the baseline for most commercial properties. However, some system types — particularly dry pipe systems common in Arizona cold-storage and warehouse facilities — require more frequent attention.

What Happens During an NFPA 25 Inspection?

A proper NFPA 25 inspection is more than a visual walkthrough. A qualified inspector will:

  • Visually inspect all accessible sprinkler heads for corrosion, paint, obstructions, and proper orientation
  • Check all control valves for position (open/closed) and accessibility
  • Test the main drain and record static and residual pressures
  • Inspect the fire department connection (FDC) for caps, clapper, and obstructions
  • Test alarm devices including water flow alarms and tamper switches
  • Review hydraulic nameplate and system design documents
  • Document all findings in a written inspection report

At Cave Fire Protection, every inspection includes a clear written report you can present to your insurance carrier, fire marshal, or property management company.

Arizona-Specific Considerations

Arizona's climate creates some unique challenges for fire protection systems:

  • Heat: Extreme summer temperatures can stress components, particularly in attics and unconditioned spaces. Sprinkler heads rated for high-temperature environments are often required.
  • Dry pipe systems: Warehouses and some commercial spaces use dry pipe systems that must be properly maintained to avoid false trips from heat fluctuations.
  • Backflow preventer testing: Arizona requires annual backflow preventer testing by a licensed tester. This is often bundled with sprinkler inspections but is a separate regulatory requirement.
  • Corrosion: Hard water and high mineral content in Phoenix-area water can accelerate internal pipe corrosion — an issue NFPA 25's five-year internal inspection is designed to catch.

What Happens If You're Not Compliant?

Non-compliance with NFPA 25 can have serious consequences:

  • Failed fire marshal inspections and potential occupancy issues
  • Insurance claim denials if a fire occurs and the system wasn't properly maintained
  • Personal liability for property managers and building owners
  • Fines from the Arizona State Fire Marshal or local jurisdiction

How Often Should You Schedule?

For most commercial properties in the Phoenix Valley, we recommend scheduling your annual NFPA 25 inspection at the same time each year — typically before your property insurance renewal or before any scheduled fire marshal inspections. This gives you time to address any deficiencies before they become compliance issues.
